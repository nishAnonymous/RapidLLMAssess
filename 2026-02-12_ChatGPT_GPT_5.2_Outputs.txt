A natural Ojibwe (Anishinaabemowin) translation is:

**Gwayak g'bizindawaa.**

### Breakdown of the grammar

* **gwayak** – an adverb meaning *carefully, correctly, properly*
* **g-** – a prefix marking a **second-person object** (“you”)
* **bizindaw** – verb root meaning *to listen to someone* (a transitive animate verb, since “you” is animate)
* **-aa** – a direct theme sign used when a **1st person subject acts on a 2nd person object**

So structurally:

> gwayak (carefully) + g-bizindaw-aa
> carefully + I-listen-to-you

Ojibwe verbs encode both subject and object within the verb itself. Because this is a **1 → 2** action (“I” acting on “you”), the special direct ending **-aa** is used.

### Literal sense:

“I carefully listen to you.”

Dialect note: Ojibwe varies by region, so spelling and pronunciation may differ slightly (e.g., *bizindaawaa*, *bizindawaa*, etc.), but the grammatical structure remains the same.

A natural way to say this in **Southwestern Ojibwe (Anishinaabemowin)** is:

**Ningii-ayaaw gagwewin, niwii-nibaa.**

---

## Breakdown and Grammatical Explanation

### 1. **Ningii-ayaaw gagwewin**

* **ni-** = first person singular prefix (“I”)
* **-gii-** = past or completed aspect marker (often used conversationally to indicate a present state that has come about)
* **ayaaw** = “to be” (a stative verb meaning “to be in a state”)
* **gagwewin** = “tiredness” or “fatigue”

Literally:

> “I am (in a state of) tiredness.”
> More naturally: **“I am tired.”**

In Ojibwe, many feelings or conditions are expressed as being “in a state of” something rather than using adjectives like in English.

---

### 2. **Niwii-nibaa**

* **ni-** = first person singular (“I”)
* **-wii-** = future or intention marker (“going to,” “will”)
* **nibaa** = “to sleep”

Literally:

> “I am going to sleep” or “I will sleep.”

The **-wii-** marker expresses intention or future action.

---

## Notes on Variation

Ojibwe has multiple dialects (e.g., Southwestern, Eastern, Northern), and wording can vary slightly depending on region. Speakers may also use slightly different expressions for “I am tired,” depending on context (for example, emphasizing exhaustion vs. simple sleepiness).

A natural way to say **“We are ending the meeting”** in Anishinaabemowin (Ojibwe)** is:

> **Giishpinitaa’imin gagwe-giizhigad.**

However, a clearer and more commonly structured phrasing would be:

> **Gaye gagwe-giizhigad gagwe-giishpinitaa’imin.**

That said, the most natural and widely acceptable translation (depending on dialect) would be:

> **Giinawind gagwe-giizhigad giishpinitaa’imin.**

---

## A More Standard and Clear Translation

A more grammatically transparent and commonly accepted translation would be:

> **Giinawind gagwe-giizhigad giishpinitaa’imin.**

### Breakdown:

* **Giinawind** — *We* (exclusive “we,” meaning we but not including the listener; Ojibwe distinguishes inclusive and exclusive we)
* **gagwe-giizhigad** — *meeting* (literally something like “gathered day/event” — terms vary by dialect; in many communities “maamawi-giizhigad” or English loanwords may also be used)
* **giishpinitaa’imin** — *we are ending it*

  * **giishpinitaa-** = to end/finish something (transitive verb)
  * **-imin** = 1st person plural ending (“we”)

---

## Grammar Explanation

Ojibwe is a **verb-centered (verb-heavy) language**, and verbs carry a lot of the meaning.

### 1. Person Marking

The verb ending **-imin** marks:

* First person plural (we)
* Acting on an inanimate object (the meeting)

Because "meeting" is grammatically inanimate, the verb uses a **Transitive Inanimate (VTI)** form.

### 2. Word Order

Ojibwe word order is relatively flexible. The meaning is carried by verb morphology, not strict word order. So you may hear:

* **Giinawind giishpinitaa’imin gagwe-giizhigad.**
* **Gagwe-giizhigad giishpinitaa’imin.**

Both can be correct.

### 3. Inclusive vs Exclusive “We”

If you want to include everyone present (including the listener), you might use:

* **Giishpinitaa’imin** (if inclusive, depending on dialect marking)

Some dialects mark inclusive/exclusive directly in the verb, others rely on context.

---

## A Simpler and Very Natural Version (Common in Meetings)

Often speakers simply say:

> **Giishpinitaa’imin.**
> *“We’re ending it.”*

Because context makes it clear that “it” refers to the meeting.
